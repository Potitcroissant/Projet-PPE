    Beyond the medical gaze: Practicing Psychologically Informed Healthcare

    Dr Liza Morton calls for a little ‘R.E.S.P.E.C.T’…

    11 November 2025

    Share this page

    I've depended on cutting-edge cardiology care since birth, in the late 70s. My care was survival-focused, with little attention given to the psychological and emotional impacts. As a teenager, I searched in vain for books on the psychological impact of growing up with a serious health condition; a gap that motivated my eventual career as a psychologist, researcher, advocate and author.  

    Chronic health condition (CHCs) affect approximately one-third of the global population (WHO, 2023) and can impact on daily functioning, relationships, self-esteem, parenting and life choices, increasing vulnerability to depression, anxiety and post-traumatic stress. Healthcare professionals also face occupational challenges to their wellbeing which can result in burnout, compassion fatigue and vicarious trauma, negatively impacting on patient care. Yet, current healthcare policy and practice poorly addresses psychological and emotional wellbeing, for patients or practitioners.

    This 'medical gaze' is a false economy which not only leads to distress but contributes to poor medical adherence, missed appointments, maladaptive coping strategies (e.g. alcohol abuse, smoking, risk taking), medical error and poor decision making, high staff turnover and absences. My new book, Beyond the Medical Gaze: Practicing Psychologically Informed Healthcare, provides an evidence-based approach that embeds contemporary understanding in psychological and emotional health into healthcare practice. 

    Drawing on contemporary theories such as Polyvagal Theory, attachment theory, compassion-based, and trauma-informed approaches, I seek to offer a comprehensive framework for healthcare practitioners, policymakers, and educators incorporating reflective exercises, real-life testimonials, case studies, examples of innovative practice and research, and practical handouts. A final chapter is provided for therapists working with this client population or supporting healthcare teams. The core principles of R.E.S.P.E.C.T. framework provides a lens to consider healthcare from a Psychologically Informed approach, as I'll now outline.

    Relational safety…

    … through compassion focused healthcare delivery, leadership and teams.

    When someone is unwell and in need of medical attention, it can be incredibly stressful for them and their loved ones and it is natural for them to look to others for support. As social beings we have evolved to build trusting relationships with others from a neurological foundation of psychological safety. We seek signals of safety through compassion via vocal tone, facial expressions, body language, trust signals and reciprocity, especially during times of adversity (Porges, 2022). While studies suggest that survival rates increase when doctors make eye contact and provide a reassuring handshake to patients (Field et al., 2010) shortcomings have been reported in health systems due to a 'compassion crisis' in healthcare delivery. Compassionate communication through active listening, co-regulation, consistency of care, techniques for delivering difficult news, establishing trust, age appropriate and neuro-affirmative communication should be an essential component of training for all healthcare professionals.  

    Empowerment…

    … by supporting self-management, healthcare literacy and by challenging disempowering aspects of healthcare.

    Self-management (medical, behavioural and emotional) promotes psychological safety among those dealing with chronic illness. This includes partnership working between patient and medical teams, shared decision making, informing and engaging patients in making choices whilst being mindful that in the 'patient role' many of us feel more vulnerable. Providing accessible information, taking concerns seriously and promoting dignity can facilitate empowerment and healthcare literacy. Challenging disempowering aspects of care (e.g. patient waiting, backless gowns, epistemic injustice, holding and restraint) can also support wellbeing and mitigate medically related trauma. 

    Soothing presence…

    … of caregivers across the healthcare journey.

    Some of my most difficult childhood memories are of being wheeled away by a team in green scrubs to go to the operating theater, where my mum was not allowed. The protective presence of the primary care giver is essential to minimise the impact of early adversity, provide comfort and to support normal development and secure attachment. Yet, there can be medical interruptions to the child's innate need for responsive caregivers, touch, connection, belonging, consistency, validation, play and learning. Recurrent medical trauma can impact on emotional development, impairing the development of self-soothing and emotional regulation, across the lifespan. Promoting the soothing presence of loved ones needs to be supported across healthcare practice. For example, studies demonstrate that parental holding of infants improves haemodynamics in the cardiac ICU (Elhoff et al, 2023) while Kangaroo care reduces mortality, infection, length of hospital stay and pain responses in preterm babies (Campbell-Yeo et al, 2022).

    Psychological safety (Polyvagal informed)…

    … by validating a normal response to adverse circumstances, incorporating coping strategies throughout healthcare delivery and by supporting wellbeing for healthcare staff.

    Polyvagal Theory offers a 'Science of safety' proposing that we constantly evaluate our world for threat and safety, termed Neuroception. We have evolved a hierarchical 'defense' system to this end (Porges, 2022). By paying attention to the patient's voice tone, facial expressions, and body language healthcare professionals can better understand and validate their response and help to shift this through containment and co-regulation.  

    To this end, it is important to validate normal reactions to distress and pain whilst being careful about rewarding emotional suppression (e.g. 'Bravery' stickers and 'hero' costumes). Healthcare professionals can facilitate coping strategies such as a cuddle from parent, humour, distraction, anesthetic cream, stress reduction exercises, emotional preparation for procedures, a supportive visit from hospital chaplain / specialist nurse, and by screening mental health and referring on to further support as indicated. 

    It is important for healthcare organisations to promote psychological safety for staff too, through compassionate leadership which promotes healthy boundaries, self-care, self-compassion, safe spaces to process and share difficult feelings and understanding, recognising and treating burnout, vicarious trauma and compassion fatigue (e.g. using Wellness Action Plans).

    Environments…

    … that better support healing, recovery, rest and wellbeing.

    Healing healthcare environments are fundamental to rest and recovery, yet clinical environments can be noisy and bright whilst lacking in privacy. Excessive sound and noise in the Intensive Care Unit has been linked with adverse and potentially preventable patient outcomes, staff errors and dissatisfaction (Tronstad et al., 2024) while ward routines often arranged to suit healthcare professionals rather than patients' needs for sleep and rest. There are many changes that could be made to promote calm and privacy while limiting disruptions to sleep. Further, incorporation of green spaces, neuro-affirmative spaces and age-appropriate care (e.g. spaces for teenagers) could also improve patient wellbeing.

    Culturally sensitive healthcare provision…

    … that tackles ableism, recognizing health inequalities and intersectionality.

     It is essential to recognise that people with CHCs have experienced social exclusion, segregation and stigma throughout history. As inherently social beings, exclusion from our social group can activate the threat system, leading to panic, anger, and shame (Gilbert, 2018). Further, CHCs are more prevalent among lower socioeconomic and other marginalised groups while people living with CHCs face disparities in income, education, employment and representation (Bogart, 2020). Children with CHCs, can face exclusion from physical education and play and on average, perform worse in school  (Jay et al, 2023). Culturally sensitive healthcare is a vehicle for decreasing health disparities by recognizing, respecting and responding to the cultural needs of individuals and tackling systemic stigma within healthcare systems.

    Trauma informed practice and environments

    Each time I attend a healthcare setting, in my mind, I can momentarily flit back to being a child again. I feel butterflies in my stomach, catch my breath, feel my body tense and struggle to assert myself. Being trauma informed involves recognising when someone has been affected by trauma and collaboratively adjusting your work to consider this. For example, feeling safe has also been found to improve healing and recovery during maternity care of women who have experienced childhood sexual trauma, while feeling unsafe with professionals could be experienced as retraumatization (Montgomery, 2013). Trauma-informed organisations embed the principles of safety, compassion, trustworthiness, choice, collaboration and empowerment into all aspects of their work and consider the needs of healthcare workers in responding to people affected by trauma to prevent vicarious trauma.  

    • Dr Liza Morton is a Counselling Psychologist / Senior Lecturer in Applied Psychology, Glasgow Caledonian University.
    • This piece is adapted from Beyond the Medical Gaze: Practicing Psychologically Informed Healthcare, Oxford University Press (2025).
